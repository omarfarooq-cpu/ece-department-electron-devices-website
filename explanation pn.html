1. Semiconductor Fundamentals

What is a Semiconductor?

A semiconductor is a material whose electrical conductivity lies between that of a conductor and an insulator. Its conductivity can be controlled by adding impurities or by applying external voltage, temperature, or light.

The most commonly used semiconductor materials are:

  • Silicon (Si)
  • Germanium (Ge)

2. Intrinsic Semiconductor

An intrinsic semiconductor is a pure semiconductor without any impurities. The number of electrons is equal to the number of holes.

At absolute zero temperature, intrinsic semiconductors behave like insulators. When temperature increases, electrons gain energy and move from the valence band to the conduction band, increasing conductivity.

Characteristics

  • Pure semiconductor material
  • Equal number of electrons and holes
  • Low electrical conductivity

3. Extrinsic Semiconductor

Extrinsic semiconductors are obtained by adding impurity atoms to intrinsic semiconductors. This process is called doping.

Doping increases the number of charge carriers and improves conductivity.

Types of Extrinsic Semiconductors

N-Type Semiconductor

  • Doped with pentavalent atoms (Phosphorus, Arsenic)
  • Extra electrons are produced
  • Electrons are majority carriers

P-Type Semiconductor

  • Doped with trivalent atoms (Boron, Gallium)
  • Holes are created
  • Holes are majority carriers

4. PN Junction Formation

A PN junction is formed when P-type semiconductor material is joined with N-type semiconductor material.

When the junction is formed:

  • Electrons move from N-region to P-region
  • Holes move from P-region to N-region
  • Charge recombination occurs near the junction

This creates a region with no mobile charge carriers called the depletion region.

5. Forward Bias

Forward bias is obtained when the P-side of the diode is connected to the positive terminal of the battery and the N-side is connected to the negative terminal.

In forward bias condition:

  • Depletion region becomes thinner
  • Barrier potential decreases
  • Current begins to flow through the diode

Typical forward voltage values:

  • Silicon diode → 0.7 V
  • Germanium diode → 0.3 V

6. Reverse Bias

Reverse bias occurs when the P-side is connected to the negative terminal and the N-side is connected to the positive terminal of the battery.

Effects of reverse bias:

  • Depletion region becomes wider
  • Barrier potential increases
  • Very small leakage current flows

7. Key Terms (Definitions)

Barrier Potential

The barrier potential is the potential difference across the depletion region of a PN junction. It is typically around 0.7 V for silicon diodes and 0.3 V for germanium diodes. The barrier potential prevents the flow of charge carriers across the junction under equilibrium conditions.

Depletion Region

The depletion region is the area around the PN junction where mobile charge carriers are depleted. It acts as an insulator and prevents current flow when the diode is reverse biased. The width of the depletion region changes with applied voltage, becoming narrower in forward bias and wider in reverse bias.

Leakage Current

Leakage current is the small current that flows through a diode under reverse bias conditions. It is primarily due to the movement of charge carriers across the depletion region.

Breakdown Voltage

Breakdown voltage is the reverse voltage at which a diode begins to conduct a large current due to breakdown mechanisms such as Zener breakdown or avalanche breakdown. It is an important parameter for diodes used in voltage regulation and protection circuits.

Forward Voltage Drop

Forward voltage drop is the voltage required to forward bias a diode and allow current to flow. It is typically around 0.7 V for silicon diodes and 0.3 V for germanium diodes. This voltage drop must be overcome for the diode to conduct in the forward direction.

Drift Current

Drift current is the current that flows due to the drift of charge carriers in an electric field. It is one of the components of the total current in a semiconductor device.

Drift Velocity

Drift velocity is the average velocity of charge carriers in a semiconductor material under the influence of an electric field. It is directly proportional to the electric field and the mobility of the charge carriers.

8. Diode V–I Characteristics

The V–I characteristic curve represents the relationship between the voltage applied across the diode and the resulting current.

Important Regions

  • Forward region
  • Reverse region
  • Breakdown region

Forward Region

In the forward region, the diode conducts current after overcoming the barrier potential. The current increases exponentially with voltage.

Reverse Region

In the reverse region, the diode blocks current flow, allowing only a small leakage current to pass.

Breakdown Region

In the breakdown region, the diode conducts a large current due to breakdown mechanisms, which can damage the diode if not properly controlled.

9. Zener Diode

A Zener diode is a special type of diode designed to operate in the breakdown region. It is heavily doped to have a low breakdown voltage, allowing it to regulate voltage in circuits.

Zener diodes are commonly used in voltage regulation applications, where they maintain a constant output voltage despite variations in input voltage or load conditions.

10. Biasing of Zener Diodes

Forward Bias

In forward bias, a Zener diode behaves like a regular diode, allowing current to flow after overcoming the forward voltage drop.

Reverse Bias

In reverse bias, a Zener diode remains non-conductive until the applied voltage reaches the Zener breakdown voltage. Once this voltage is reached, the diode conducts in reverse, maintaining a constant voltage across its terminals.

V-I Characteristics of Zener Diode

The V-I characteristic curve of a Zener diode shows that in forward bias, it behaves like a regular diode, while in reverse bias, it remains non-conductive until the Zener breakdown voltage is reached, at which point it conducts in reverse to maintain a constant voltage.

11. Breakdown Mechanisms

Zener Breakdown

Zener breakdown occurs at low reverse voltage in heavily doped diodes. The strong electric field causes electrons to move across the depletion region.

Application: Voltage regulation.

Avalanche Breakdown

Avalanche breakdown occurs at high reverse voltage. Electrons collide with atoms and generate more charge carriers, causing a sudden increase in current.

12. Energy Bands in PN Junction

In a PN junction, the energy bands of the P-type and N-type materials align to form a continuous energy band structure. The conduction band of the N-type material is higher than that of the P-type material, while the valence band of the P-type material is higher than that of the N-type material. This alignment creates a potential barrier at the junction, which affects the movement of charge carriers and determines the electrical behavior of the diode.

13. Rectifiers

Rectifiers are circuits that convert alternating current (AC) into direct current (DC).

Types of Rectifiers

  • Half Wave Rectifier
  • Full Wave Rectifier
  • Bridge Rectifier

Half Wave Rectifier

1. Definition

A half wave rectifier uses a single diode to allow only one half of the AC waveform to pass through, resulting in a pulsating DC output.

2. Components Used

Typical half wave rectifier circuit contains: AC Voltage Source / Transformer PN Junction Diode Load Resistor (RL)

3. Circuit Diagram Explanation

In the circuit: The transformer provides AC voltage. The diode acts as a switch. The load resistor (RL) receives the rectified output. The diode is connected in series with the load resistor.

4. Working Principle

The operation depends on the two half cycles of the AC input.

(1) Positive Half Cycle

During the positive half cycle, the anode of the diode becomes positive relative to the cathode. The diode becomes forward biased. Current flows through the circuit. Therefore: Current flows through RL Output voltage appears across the load. Result: The positive half of the AC waveform appears at the output.

(2) Negative Half Cycle

During the negative half cycle, the anode becomes negative relative to the cathode. The diode becomes reverse biased. No current flows through the circuit. Result: The negative half of the waveform is blocked.

5. Input and Output Waveforms

Input waveform: Complete sinusoidal AC wave. Output waveform: Only positive half cycles. Thus the output becomes pulsating DC.

Full Wave Rectifier

A full wave rectifier uses two or more diodes to allow both halves of the AC waveform to pass through, resulting in a smoother DC output compared to a half wave rectifier. There are two common types of full wave rectifiers: the center-tapped full wave rectifier and the bridge rectifier.

Types of Full Wave Rectifiers

  • Center-Tapped Full Wave Rectifier
  • Bridge Rectifier

Center-Tapped Full Wave Rectifier

1. Definition

A center-tapped full wave rectifier uses a transformer with a center-tapped secondary winding and two diodes. Each diode conducts during alternate half cycles of the AC input, allowing both halves of the waveform to be rectified.

2. Construction

A centre tapped transformer consists of:

Primary winding

Secondary winding split into two equal halves Centre tap connection at the middle of secondary If the total secondary voltage is Vs, then: Voltage between centre tap and one end = Vs / 2 Voltage between centre tap and other end = Vs / 2 The two halves produce equal magnitude but opposite polarity voltages. Example: If secondary voltage = 12V End 1 to centre tap = 6V End 2 to centre tap = 6V

3. Working Principle

The centre tapped transformer works based on mutual induction.

Step 1 – AC Input

When AC voltage is applied to the primary winding, an alternating magnetic flux is produced in the transformer core.

Step 2 – Induced Voltage

This changing magnetic flux induces alternating voltages in the secondary winding.

Step 3 – Centre Tap Function

Because the secondary winding is split into two equal parts: Each half produces voltage during alternate half cycles The centre tap acts as a common reference point This feature is mainly used in Full Wave Rectifiers.

4. Operation in Full Wave Rectifier

During the positive half cycle:

Upper secondary winding becomes positive First diode conducts Current flows through load

During the negative half cycle:

Lower secondary winding becomes positive Second diode conducts Current flows through load in the same direction Thus both half cycles are used to produce full wave rectification.

13. Clippers

Clippers are circuits that remove or clip a portion of an input waveform without distorting the remaining part of the signal.

Types:

  • Positive clipper
  • Negative clipper
  • Biased clipper

14. Clampers

Clampers are circuits that shift the entire waveform upward or downward without altering its shape.

  • Positive clamper
  • Negative clamper
